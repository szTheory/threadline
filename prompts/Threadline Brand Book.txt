Threadline Brand Book

1) Brand foundation

Brand name

Threadline

Why the name works

Threadline suggests the connecting line that lets someone follow what happened across time, systems, requests, actors, and changes. It feels more distinctive than literal audit names, while still implying traceability, continuity, and narrative clarity.

It is a strong fit for the product because the library is not only about storing changes. It is about connecting:

* action to change,
* request to transaction,
* actor to outcome,
* event to evidence,
* history to explanation.

Core brand idea

Threadline makes system history followable.

Brand thesis

Threadline is the audit platform for Phoenix, Ecto, and PostgreSQL that turns low-level change capture into a coherent, explainable history.

One-line positioning

Threadline is the audit platform that connects every change to the action, context, and story around it.

Category framing

Use these phrases consistently:

* audit platform
* audit infrastructure
* audit trail for Phoenix/Ecto/Postgres
* action-and-change history
* explainable audit timeline
* context-rich audit system

Avoid framing it as:

* just a log viewer
* just a Carbonite UI
* compliance software
* SIEM
* event sourcing
* generic observability

3) Strategic positioning

What Threadline is really selling

Not merely capture.

Not merely a timeline.

Not merely compliance.

Threadline sells confidence through connected history.

Key differentiators to reinforce in messaging

1. Capture plus meaning — not just row changes, but actions, actors, reasons, and request/job context.
2. Explainable by default — designed so humans can answer “what happened?” quickly.
3. Operationally credible — health checks, retention, redaction, exports, coverage.
4. Native to Phoenix/Ecto/Postgres — feels built for the stack, not adapted to it.
5. Trustworthy without becoming opaque — SQL-friendly, inspectable, composable.

Emotional territory

The feeling should be:

* “I can follow this.”
* “This gives me confidence.”
* “This is serious, but not intimidating.”
* “This helps in a real incident, not just in a demo.”

5) Messaging framework

Brand narrative

Systems change constantly. Most tools either capture too little, or capture enough but make it hard to understand. Threadline exists to connect the raw truth of change with the human context around it, so teams can follow what happened with confidence.

Elevator pitch

Threadline is a batteries-included audit platform for Phoenix, Ecto, and PostgreSQL. It captures row-level changes, connects them to actions and request context, and turns that history into readable timelines, diffs, and operator workflows.

10-second description

Follow every important change.

30-second description

Threadline gives Phoenix and Ecto teams an audit system they can actually use. It captures changes at the database layer, connects them to actors, actions, and request context, and makes the result explorable through timelines, diffs, filtering, and operational tooling.

Tagline options

Primary recommendation:

* Follow what happened.

Strong alternatives:

* History, with context.
* Every change has a thread.
* Connected audit history.
* Traceable by design.
* The line through every change.
* Follow the full story.

Homepage headline options

* Follow what happened.
* Audit history you can actually follow.
* Every change, connected to its context.
* From row changes to readable history.
* A clear line through every action and change.

Supporting subhead options

* Capture row-level changes. Link them to actions, actors, and request context. Explore everything through readable timelines and diffs.
* Threadline turns low-level database changes into clear, explainable history for Phoenix and Ecto teams.
* Built for teams that need trustworthy audit capture and an operator experience that makes sense under pressure.

Messaging pillars

1. Trustworthy capture
Hard to bypass. Grounded in real system changes.

2. Context-rich history
Who did it, why, in what request or job, and what it affected.

3. Readable exploration
Timelines, diffs, filters, drill-down, and exports that answer real questions.

4. Operational confidence
Coverage checks, health signals, retention, redaction, and upgrade ergonomics.

6) Brand voice

Voice summary

Threadline speaks like a calm senior engineer with strong product taste.

Tone attributes

* clear
* direct
* intelligent
* unhurried
* grounded
* evidence-oriented
* helpful
* low-ego

Tone spectrum

Prefer:

* precise over clever
* plainspoken over buzzwordy
* confident over swaggering
* thoughtful over promotional
* calm over urgent
* technical over salesy

Writing principles

Say what it does plainly.
Avoid abstract claims when concrete ones are stronger.

Make technical ideas readable.
Explain without oversimplifying.

Respect the user’s intelligence.
Do not over-market obvious capabilities.

Keep it composable.
Use language that can scale from docs to UI to enterprise pages.

Be exact about boundaries.
Distinguish changes from actions, requests from transactions, audit history from database activity auditing.

Brand voice examples

Instead of:

* Revolutionary audit intelligence for modern teams.

Prefer:

* Capture changes, connect them to context, and follow the full history.

Instead of:

* Enterprise-grade observability for governance workflows.

Prefer:

* Understand what changed, who initiated it, and why.

Instead of:

* Compliance-ready trust infrastructure.

Prefer:

* A trustworthy audit system for real debugging, support, and review.

Voice by surface

Docs
Sober, explanatory, practical.

Marketing site
Clear, aspirational, but still technical.

Admin UI
Plain and legible. Prioritize certainty and actionability.

Launch posts
Opinionated and crisp. Teach the category while introducing the product.

Error states
Specific, transparent, fix-oriented.

9) Logo direction

Core logo idea

A continuous line or set of layered lines that suggest connection, sequence, and reconstruction.

Symbol directions

Direction A: Continuous thread
A single line that passes through a series of points and resolves into a subtle T or TL monogram.

Direction B: Parallel strands
Multiple thin lines traveling together, diverging briefly, then reconverging. Good metaphor for action, transaction, and change.

Direction C: Stitch mark
A restrained stitched motif that feels technical, not crafty. Better for editorial assets than the primary logo.

Direction D: Cross-section linework
Layered contour or stratified lines, implying history revealed over time.

Wordmark guidance

Use a crisp geometric or neo-grotesk sans wordmark with tight spacing and slightly custom letter relationships around Th, ea, or dl to create ownability.

Logo traits

* horizontal first
* strong at small sizes
* works in one color
* legible in favicon form
* line-based rather than bulky
* should not rely on security tropes

Logo don’ts

* no shield icons
* no magnifying glass
* no database cylinder as the main motif
* no checkmark compliance imagery
* no chain links
* no fingerprint imagery

11) Typography

Typography philosophy

The type system should feel technical, contemporary, and highly readable. It should work equally well for product UI, docs, dashboards, and marketing.

Primary sans

Geist
Use as the primary brand and interface typeface.

Why:

* modern and technical without feeling generic,
* excellent on screens,
* strong for product UI and developer tools,
* clean enough for docs and marketing.

Fallbacks:

* Inter
* system-ui
* sans-serif

Secondary sans option

Inter
Acceptable alternate when Geist is unavailable or when broad tooling compatibility matters.

Monospace

IBM Plex Mono
Use for code, request IDs, diff values, table names, event names, and metadata labels.

Fallbacks:

* JetBrains Mono
* ui-monospace
* SFMono-Regular
* Menlo
* monospace

Optional editorial accent

Newsreader or Instrument Serif
Use only on marketing pages or launch assets if you want a slightly more distinctive editorial note.

Rule:

* never use serif in the admin UI,
* keep serif usage restrained,
* do not let the brand become literary or artisanal.

Type roles

Display / hero
Geist Medium or Semibold, tight tracking, high line clarity.

Section headings
Geist Semibold.

Body
Geist Regular.

Code / metadata
IBM Plex Mono Regular or Medium.

Typographic character

* restrained
* confident
* not overly rounded
* not sterile
* strong numerals
* excellent code pairing

Rules of use

* Use mono to signal data, structure, and identifiers.
* Do not overuse mono in marketing copy.
* Keep UI typography compact but breathable.
* Favor optical cleanliness over stylistic experimentation.

12) Layout and composition

Layout principles

* Strong grid
* Calm spacing
* Clear visual hierarchy
* Dense where needed, never cluttered
* Structured panels and rails
* Consistent alignment

Shape language

* rounded but not bubbly
* subtle corner radius
* thin dividers
* linear motifs
* layered panels
* cards that feel infrastructural, not playful

Density guidance

Admin UI can be moderately dense, but always preserve:

* readable line length,
* obvious grouping,
* strong table legibility,
* clear hierarchy between action, context, and raw changes.
